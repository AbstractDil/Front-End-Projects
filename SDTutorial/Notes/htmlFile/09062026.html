Determinants & Fundamental Properties

  • 1. $\det(A) = \det(A^T) \implies |A^T| = |A|$
  • 2. If $A$ is a square matrix of order $n \times n$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$ is any scalar, then: $\det(cA) = c^n \cdot \det(A)$
  • 3. $\det(AB) = \det(A) \cdot \det(B)$
Important Note on Matrix Multiplication
In general, matrix multiplication is non-commutative: $AB \neq BA$.
However, their determinants satisfy absolute equality: $$\mathbf{|AB| = |BA|}$$
Proof Hook: $|AB| = |A||B| = |B||A| = |BA|$ (Since scalar values commute freely).
  • 4. $\det(A+B) \neq \det(A) + \det(B)$ in general.
Illustrative Example
Let $A$ be a $3 \times 3$ matrix such that $\det(A) = 5$. Find $\det(-4A)$.
$$\det(-4A) = (-4)^3 \cdot \det(A)$$ $$\implies (-64) \times 5 = \mathbf{-320}$$

Adjoint and Inverse Matrix Identities

Let $A$ be a square matrix of order $n \times n$. The following identities hold firmly:

  • 5. $\text{adj}(A^T) = (\text{adj } A)^T$
  • 6. $\text{adj}(cA) = c^{n-1} \cdot \text{adj } A$
  • 7. $\text{adj}(\text{adj } A) = |A|^{n-2} \cdot A$
  • 8. $A \cdot (\text{adj } A) = (\text{adj } A) \cdot A = |A| \cdot I$
  • 9. $|\text{adj } A| = |A|^{n-1}$
  • 10. $|\text{adj}(\text{adj } A)| = |A|^{(n-1)^2}$
  • 11. $|A^{-1}| = \frac{1}{|A|}$
  • 12. $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{|A|} \cdot \text{adj } A$

Orthogonal Matrices

A square matrix $A$ of order $n \times n$ is defined as an orthogonal matrix if and only if:

$$A \cdot A^T = A^T \cdot A = I_n$$

Key Algebraic Properties:

  • Taking determinants on both sides: $|A \cdot A^T| = |I| \implies |A| \cdot |A^T| = 1 \implies |A|^2 = 1 \implies \mathbf{|A| = \pm 1}$.
  • If $\mathbf{|A| = 1}$, the matrix is specifically classified as a Proper Orthogonal Matrix.
The 2D Standard Rotation Matrix Case
Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\alpha & -\sin\alpha \\ \sin\alpha & \cos\alpha \end{bmatrix}$. This matrix is proper orthogonal ($|A| = 1$).

General Matrix Power Property: $$A^n = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(n\alpha) & -\sin(n\alpha) \\ \sin(n\alpha) & \cos(n\alpha) \end{bmatrix}$$
Vector Interpretation (Column Vectors): Let columns be vectors $\vec{c}_1 = [\cos\alpha, \sin\alpha]^T$ and $\vec{c}_2 = [-\sin\alpha, \cos\alpha]^T$.
1. Orthogonality Check: $\vec{c}_1 \cdot \vec{c}_2 = \cos\alpha(-\sin\alpha) + \sin\alpha\cos\alpha = 0$
2. Normality Check: $|\vec{c}_1| = \sqrt{\cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha} = 1$, and $|\vec{c}_2| = \sqrt{(-\sin\alpha)^2 + \cos^2\alpha} = 1$

Idempotent Matrices

A square matrix $A$ is classified as idempotent if and only if squaring the matrix yields itself:

$$A^2 = A$$
Theorem: Simultaneous Idempotency
If two matrices $A$ and $B$ satisfy the relations $\mathbf{AB = B}$ and $\mathbf{BA = A}$, then both $A$ and $B$ are independently idempotent: $$A^2 = A \quad \text{and} \quad B^2 = B$$ Furthermore, their sums satisfy the relation: $\mathbf{A^2 + B^2 = A + B}$
Theorem: Identity Complement Matrix
If $A$ is an idempotent matrix ($A^2 = A$), then its identity complement matrix $(I - A)$ is also cleanly idempotent.
Proof Verification: $$(I - A)^2 = (I - A)(I - A) = I^2 - I\cdot A - A\cdot I + A^2$$ $$= I - 2A + A^2$$ $$\text{Since } A^2 = A \implies = I - 2A + A = \mathbf{I - A}$$

Standard Cyclic Determinant Equations

These structural cyclic patterns frequently surface in evaluations and should be evaluated directly:

i. Type 1: Standard Linear-Square Vandermonde Form
$$\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ a & b & c \\ a^2 & b^2 & c^2 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{(a-b)(b-c)(c-a)}$$
ii. Type 2: Square-Cube Form (Linear Row Excluded)
$$\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ a^2 & b^2 & c^2 \\ a^3 & b^3 & c^3 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{(a-b)(b-c)(c-a)(ab+bc+ca)}$$
iii. Type 3: Linear-Cube Form (Square Row Excluded)
$$\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ a & b & c \\ a^3 & b^3 & c^3 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{(a-b)(b-c)(c-a)(a+b+c)}$$
iv. Type 4: Pure Cyclic Symmetric Cubic Structure
$$\begin{vmatrix} a & b & c \\ b & c & a \\ c & a & b \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{-(a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc)}$$

Characteristic Equations via Cayley-Hamilton Theorem

Every square matrix $A$ structurally satisfies its own unique characteristic equation, yielding critical shortcuts:

Shortcut Formula for $2 \times 2$ Matrices
$$A^2 - (\text{tr } A)A + (\det A)I = \mathbf{O}$$
*Note: $\text{tr } A$ (Trace of $A$) = Sum of principal diagonal elements $= a_{11} + a_{22}$.
Shortcut Formula for $3 \times 3$ Matrices
$$A^3 - (\text{tr } A)A^2 + (A_{11} + A_{22} + A_{33})A - (\det A)I = \mathbf{O}$$
Where $A_{11}, A_{22}, A_{33}$ represent the principle diagonal cofactors of matrix $A$.
Alternatively defined as: $(A_{11} + A_{22} + A_{33}) = \text{trace}(\text{adj } A)$.
